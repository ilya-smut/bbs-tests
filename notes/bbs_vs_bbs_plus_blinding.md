# Blinding BBS vs BBS+\n\nIn the **original BBS signature** (as defined by Boneh, Boyen, and Shacham in 2004), the signature consists of just two curve points: `(A, e)`. There is no scalar `s` involved in the signature itself.\n\nThe base `B` in original BBS is just:\n\n$$B = g_1 \cdot h_1^{m_1} \cdot h_2^{m_2} \dots$$\n\nIf you try to blind $m_1$ using a commitment like $C = h_1^{m_1} \cdot h_0^{\tilde{s}}$, the issuer would sign $B = g_1 \cdot C \dots$\n\nBut the resulting signature `(A, e)` would be signing a base `B` that inherently includes $h_0^{\tilde{s}}$. There is no way for the prover to "unblind" it, because the signature `(A, e)` doesn't have an `s` component to absorb the blinding factor $\tilde{s}$. The signature is permanently linked to the blinding factor you used! You would never be able to present a clean signature `(A, e)` that works for the original base $B = g_1 \cdot h_1^{m_1} \dots$, because the signature applies to $B' = B \cdot h_0^{\tilde{s}}$.\n\n### This is Why BBS+ Was Invented\n\nThis exact problem is precisely why researchers (Aun, et al. in 2016) created **BBS+**!\n\nThey realized you needed a way to securely "absorb" the blinding factor during issuance. By adding the random scalar `s` to the signature structure, and defining the base as:\n\n$$B = g_1 \cdot h_0^s \cdot h_1^{m_1} \cdot h_2^{m_2} \dots$$\n\nThey created a "sponge" (the $h_0^s$ term). The prover can introduce their blinding factor $\tilde{s}$ as part of a commitment $C = h_0^{\tilde{s}} \cdot h_1^{m_1}$, the issuer adds their own $s'$, and the structure perfectly absorbs both of them into a single, cohesive $s = \tilde{s} + s'$ that can be cleanly separated and put right into the signature tuple!\n\nThe lack of the `s` term in the original BBS scheme makes it fundamentally incapable of native, clean 2-party blinded issuance in this manner.
